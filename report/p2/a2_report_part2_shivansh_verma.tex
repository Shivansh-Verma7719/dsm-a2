\section{Shared Subset Construction (Used for Neo4j and Predictive Modelling)}

\subsection*{Why a Shared Subset Was Built}
Part 2 includes graph analytics and predictive modelling workloads that are expensive on the full Yelp dump. To keep execution feasible and reproducible on local hardware while preserving assignment complexity, a single materialized subset was built and then reused across both downstream tracks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Neo4j + GDS querying
    \item Predictive modelling dataset construction and evaluation
\end{itemize}

Using one canonical subset avoids dataset drift between tasks and ensures that model features, graph structures, and exploratory results are all computed from the same entity universe.

\subsection*{Subset Construction Pipeline (ETL)}
The subset is created by \texttt{etl/build\_shared\_subset.py}. The logic is deterministic given parameters and follows these steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Scan businesses and keep only valid candidates (non-empty state/city/categories, minimum review count, optional check-in constraint).
    \item Allocate a target business budget across top states using proportional quotas with a minimum floor per state.
    \item Within each state, select businesses in a round-robin manner across primary categories to improve category diversity.
    \item Keep reviews only for selected businesses, with a per-business cap.
    \item Keep users referenced by selected reviews.
    \item Prune each kept user's friend list to subset users only (keeps social edges coherent inside subset).
    \item Keep check-ins and tips for selected businesses.
\end{enumerate}

This produces a smaller but structurally consistent induced multi-collection/multi-graph dataset.

\subsection*{Subset Configuration and Resulting Size}
From \texttt{data/subset\_shared/subset\_manifest.json} (subset name: \texttt{shared\_v1}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Parameters:} target businesses=6000, max states=12, min businesses/state=250, min business review count=15, max reviews/business=120.
    \item \textbf{Counts:} businesses=6000, reviews=437582, users=289869, check-ins=5634, tips=147910.
\end{itemize}

State quota allocation preserved geographic spread while still emphasizing high-signal states. Largest state allocations were PA (1250) and FL (1054), with additional representation from TN, IN, MO, LA, AZ, NV, NJ, CA, ID, and AB.

\subsection*{Why This Matters for the Report}
All Neo4j and predictive modelling results reported below are executed on this shared subset namespace (\texttt{subset\_shared}), not on the full original graph, to keep conclusions internally consistent.

\section{Neo4j / Cypher Querying}

\subsection*{NQ1: PageRank on User-Business Projection}
The query projects a directed weighted graph from users to businesses using review stars as weights and runs GDS PageRank with minimum 20 iterations.

Deliverables produced by script:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Top-15 businesses by PageRank with review count and average stars
    \item Spearman correlations: PageRank vs review-count ranking; PageRank vs average-star ranking
    \item Rank-gap analysis for businesses over/under-ranked by PageRank vs simple metrics
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/neo4j/n1_top15_pagerank_bar.png}
    \caption{NQ1: Top 15 businesses by PageRank score.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/neo4j/n1_pagerank_vs_reviewcount_scatter.png}
    \caption{NQ1: PageRank score vs review count.}
\end{figure}

Key findings (\texttt{n1\_summary.json}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Spearman correlation(PageRank, review count) = \textbf{0.929}, indicating strong agreement between authority and volume.
    \item Spearman correlation(PageRank, average stars) = \textbf{0.085}, suggesting weak monotonic dependence on mean rating.
    \item Top-ranked business by PageRank: \textbf{Fiori Per Te}.
\end{itemize}

Analytical interpretation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The very high PageRank vs review-count correlation indicates that in this subset, \textbf{exposure and interaction volume dominate} influence. Businesses with many inbound user interactions absorb centrality mass regardless of sentiment.
    \item The weak PageRank vs mean-stars correlation shows that \textbf{network prominence is not equivalent to customer satisfaction}. A business can be structurally central while being poorly rated, and vice versa.
    \item Rank-gap diagnostics reveal two distinct regimes:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Over-ranked by PageRank} (large positive gap): entities such as Dent Artist Paintless Dent Removal and Better Business Bureau Serving West Florida have moderate review counts but high graph centrality, suggesting they sit in reviewer pathways that connect active user neighborhoods.
        \item \textbf{Under-ranked by PageRank} (large negative gap): high-volume brands such as Whole Foods Market and Walmart Supercenter receive many reviews but are less central in reviewer flow, consistent with broad but less tightly connected audience overlap.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

Business insights:
\begin{itemize}
    \item For recommendation/ranking strategy, a two-signal policy is preferable: centrality for discoverability and rating for quality control.
    \item For business growth, partnerships with highly connected reviewer segments may improve visibility faster than marginal rating improvements alone.
\end{itemize}

Key findings (\texttt{n2\_summary.json}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Communities with size $\geq 25$: \textbf{19}
    \item Most geographically concentrated community: \textbf{12480} with GCI \textbf{0.979}
    \item Least concentrated community: \textbf{7449} with GCI \textbf{0.171}
    \item Selected projection threshold: \texttt{min\_reviews = 10}
\end{itemize}

Analytical interpretation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item GCI spans a wide range (approximately \textbf{0.17 to 0.98}), which means the social graph contains both highly local and highly cross-region communities.
    \item The most concentrated communities are nearly single-state clusters (for example community 12480 is AB-dominant), whereas the least concentrated communities distribute review activity across multiple states with no dominant geography.
    \item Across communities, top reviewed categories repeatedly include Restaurants, Food, and Nightlife, implying that \textbf{community differentiation is more geographic than categorical}. In other words, members in different communities often consume similar category types but in different places.
\end{itemize}

Business insights:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Geo-targeted campaigns should prioritize high-GCI communities because local message relevance is likely to be strongest.
    \item Cross-state expansion and referral strategies are better aligned to low-GCI communities where social influence is less geographically concentrated.
\end{itemize}

Node Similarity is run on business nodes via shared reviewer neighborhoods. Threshold starts at 5 common reviewers and falls back to 4 or 3 if needed. Results include top-5 most saturated and top-5 most fragmented city-category combinations, excluding combinations with fewer than 5 businesses.

\begin{figure}[H]

    \caption{NQ3: Most saturated and most fragmented city-category combinations.}
\end{figure}

Key findings (\texttt{n3\_summary.json}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item NodeSimilarity configuration used: \texttt{top\_k=5}, \texttt{min\_common\_reviewers=5}
    \item Most saturated combo: \textbf{Philadelphia | Arts \& Crafts} (mean similarity \textbf{0.203})
    \item Most fragmented combo: \textbf{Indianapolis | Event Planning \& Services} (mean similarity \textbf{0.000})
\end{itemize}

The script also computes mean stars, star spread, and mean review count for the most and least saturated combinations.

Analytical interpretation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Saturation (high intra-category audience overlap) appears in narrow pockets. Example: Philadelphia | Arts \& Crafts has mean similarity \textbf{0.203}.
    \item Many city-category pairs have similarity near zero, including several Event Planning \& Services combinations. This indicates fragmented demand where different businesses draw largely distinct reviewers.
    \item Comparing the extreme pairs in \texttt{n3\_extreme\_combo\_business\_metrics.csv}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Saturated pair (Philadelphia | Arts \& Crafts): mean stars \textbf{4.08}, star standard deviation \textbf{0.845}, mean review count \textbf{112.9}.
        \item Fragmented pair (Indianapolis | Event Planning \& Services): mean stars \textbf{4.13}, star standard deviation \textbf{0.701}, mean review count \textbf{188.0}.
    \end{itemize}
    \item In this sample, the saturated market has \textbf{higher rating variance but lower average review volume}. This supports the interpretation that direct audience overlap can intensify competitive differentiation while limiting per-business attention.
\end{itemize}

Business insights:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Entering saturated categories requires sharper positioning because consumers compare overlapping options directly.
    \item Entering fragmented categories may benefit from local niche capture and channel-specific acquisition rather than direct head-to-head brand competition.
\end{itemize}

Business insights:
\begin{itemize}

    \item Category-diverse connectors are valuable for introducing new categories or cross-selling between adjacent verticals.

\subsection*{Modelling Strategy and Methods}
Train/test split follows the required stratification buckets: \{0, 1--5, 6--20, 21+\}. Candidate models were evaluated in an auto-selection pipeline:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Histogram Gradient Boosting regressors (standard and log-target variants)
    \item Poisson-loss gradient boosting variants
    \item Tweedie/Poisson GLM baselines
    \item Fast random forest baseline
    \item Hurdle model (two-stage: classify useful$>$0, then regress positive counts)
    \item Blend candidates combining complementary predictors
\end{itemize}

Model objective selection used minimum RMSE on the held-out test set. Feature importance is reported via permutation importance (on sampled test rows) for interpretability.

\subsection*{Limitation and Potential Consequence}
\textbf{Limitation:} the target is highly imbalanced with very few extreme high-useful instances (21+), and even fewer very large values. This limits tail calibration and reduces reliability for ranking the very top reviews.

\textbf{Potential consequence if deployed for ranking:} an under-calibrated model may systematically under-rank niche but high-value reviews (especially from smaller businesses or less represented communities), reinforcing popularity bias toward already visible entities.
